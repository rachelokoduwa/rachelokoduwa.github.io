Smart Fabric Advisor

What happens when a curiosity about fabric meets a curiosity about machine learning?

← Back to Portfolio

I have always been interested in fabric. Not fashion in the way most people think about it, but the actual material. Where it comes from, what makes one fabric better than another for a specific purpose, why certain fabrics work in certain climates. That has always been something I found genuinely interesting.

At the same time, I kept hearing about machine learning everywhere. Every data analytics conversation eventually circled back to ML and AI. So I got curious. I wanted to actually understand what it was, not just read about it but build something with it.

One day those two things collided. I thought about someone trying to figure out what to wear to an event and realised there was no good answer to that question online unless you already knew what you were looking for. So I simplified it: what kind of fabric is appropriate for a specific event, in a specific location, in a specific season? That question had a structure I could work with.

The Goal

Build a machine learning model that recommends the most suitable fabric for any occasion based on four inputs: city, season, event type, and weather conditions. Then wrap it in a web app anyone could actually use.

Tools used: Python (pandas, numpy, scikit-learn, matplotlib, seaborn), Streamlit, joblib

Models compared: Linear Regression, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting

Building the Dataset

There is no publicly available dataset that maps fabrics to events and climates, so I built one from scratch. This was actually one of the most interesting parts of the project because it forced me to think carefully about what actually makes a fabric suitable for a given situation.

The dataset was built across five dimensions: 13 fabric types each with properties like breathability, warmth, formality, and water resistance; 12 event types with formality levels and activity requirements; 38 cities mapped to 7 climate zones; summer and winter conditions for each zone. Combining all of these produced 2,496 unique training scenarios.

Each scenario was assigned a suitability score from 0 to 100 based on a weighted formula. Temperature comfort carried the most weight at 50% because wearing the wrong fabric for the weather is the most immediately uncomfortable problem. Formality match and activity suitability each carried 20%, and weather protection made up the remaining 10%.

The Models

I trained and compared three models to understand how they differed and why one outperformed the others.

Model Test MAE R² Score What it told me
Linear Regression 10.05 0.22 Too simple. Fabric suitability is not a straight line relationship.
Random Forest 1.10 0.99 Excellent. Builds hundreds of trees independently and averages results.
Gradient Boosting 0.46 0.998 Best in class. Builds trees sequentially, each correcting the last.

The difference between Linear Regression and the other two was stark. A linear model assumes that adding more warmth always improves the score by the same amount regardless of context. But that is not how fabric works. Warmth is great in an Arctic winter and terrible on a Dubai summer beach. The tree-based models captured those interactions naturally.

Gradient Boosting edged out Random Forest because of how it learns. Rather than building trees independently, it builds them one at a time and each new tree focuses specifically on the errors the previous ones made. With 200 boosting stages and a learning rate of 0.1, it corrected itself gradually and precisely.

Final model accuracy: 99.81% R² with a mean absolute error of 0.46 points. On average, predictions are off by less than half a point out of 100.

What the App Does

The Streamlit app takes three inputs from the user: city, season, and event type. It looks up the climate zone and weather conditions for that city and season, then runs the Gradient Boosting model across all 13 fabrics simultaneously and returns them ranked by suitability score. Each result shows a score, a rating category, and a breakdown of the fabric's key properties.

Some examples of what it recommends:

  • Winter wedding in Yellowknife, Canada at -40°C: Cashmere, Wool, Velvet at the top
  • Summer beach outing in Dubai at 40°C: Linen, Cotton, Rayon at the top
  • Summer business meeting in Toronto: Polyester, Nylon, Cotton at the top

The Hardest Part

Honestly, the hardest part was also the most enjoyable part. I had never written code this complex before. There were moments where what I was typing looked like complete nonsense to me but the results made sense, which was a strange and genuinely exciting feeling. I had Claude AI teaching me along the way which helped enormously, but the understanding had to come from me. You can not just copy code and call it learning.

There was also a compatibility issue that surfaced months later when I tried to run the app again. The model had been saved in Google Colab with one version of numpy and the new environment had a different version. The model file was essentially unreadable. The fix was to retrain the model from scratch in the new environment, which also gave me an opportunity to verify the recommendations were working correctly.

What I Learned

I came into this project knowing almost nothing about machine learning. I left it understanding the difference between linear and non-linear models, why ensemble methods outperform simple approaches, how to evaluate a model beyond just looking at accuracy, and how to deploy a working ML application to the web.

More than the technical skills though, I learned that consistency matters more than speed. This project took a full week. Some days were slow and frustrating. But showing up every day and building on what I learned the day before is what got it done. I am not a machine learning expert and I am not pretending to be. But I am in a far better place than I was before I started, and that is the whole point.

Tech Wages vs Inflation vs Income Inequality

Did tech workers really come out ahead while everyone else suffered?

← Back to Portfolio

There is a common misconception that tech workers made so much money after the pandemic that they drove up inflation while everyone else suffered. This project pulls real data directly from the Federal Reserve to find out if that claim actually holds up. The short answer is that it does not, at least not in the way most people think.

The Question

After COVID, headlines were full of stories about tech salaries exploding and Silicon Valley workers getting rich while everyone else struggled with inflation. I wanted to look at the actual data and ask a simple question: did tech workers really come out ahead, and if so by how much?

Tools used: Python (Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn), FRED API, GitHub Codespaces

Data source: Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis (FRED) — wage series by sector and CPI inflation data

The Data

I pulled 10 years of data from the Federal Reserve's free public API covering average hourly wages across five sectors and the Consumer Price Index for inflation. The sectors covered were Tech and Information, Professional Services, Healthcare, Retail, and All Workers Average.

I initially looked at the full 2015 to 2024 period but then narrowed the focus to September 2020 onwards because that is when the real tech hiring boom began. Interestingly the overall conclusion stayed the same whether you look at 10 years or just the post-COVID period, which was itself a surprising finding.

What I Found

The data tells a more nuanced story than the popular narrative suggests.

Since September 2020, every sector lost purchasing power after inflation:

  • Inflation: +22.2%
  • Professional Services: +22.5% (barely broke even)
  • Healthcare: +21.9% (lost to inflation)
  • Tech and Information: +21.3% (also lost to inflation)
  • All Workers Average: +20.9% (lost to inflation)
  • Retail: +16.9% (hit the hardest)

Key finding: Tech workers lost 0.8% of real purchasing power since September 2020. Retail workers lost 5.2%. Everyone got poorer in real terms, just at different rates.

The most surprising chart was the pain period from 2021 to 2023. During the inflation peak, tech wage growth actually collapsed to around 2.5% while inflation was running at nearly 9%. Retail and healthcare workers had higher wage growth than tech workers during that specific window, largely because those workers could not be laid off remotely and had stronger bargaining power at the time.

The absolute dollar gap tells the inequality story more clearly:

  • Tech workers earn $46.47/hr on average
  • Professional Services: $43.31/hr
  • Healthcare: $35.01/hr
  • All Workers Average: $35.69/hr
  • Retail: $24.87/hr

The gap between tech and retail workers has grown to $21.60 per hour by 2024. Even when the percentage growth rates look similar, a 1% loss on $46/hr hurts a lot less than a 5% loss on $25/hr. The inequality is not just about growth rates, it is baked into the absolute dollar difference.

A Note on the Popular Narrative

The headlines tend to focus on senior engineers at Google and Meta making $300K to $500K in total compensation. But those people are a tiny fraction of the tech workforce. The FRED data captures the average tech worker, the IT support person, the junior developer, the data analyst at a software company. Their story is much closer to everyone else's than the headlines suggest.

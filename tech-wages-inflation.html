Tech Wages vs Inflation vs Income Inequality

Did tech workers really come out ahead while everyone else suffered?

← Back to Portfolio

There is a common misconception that tech workers made so much money after the pandemic that they drove up inflation while everyone else suffered. This project pulls real data directly from the Federal Reserve to find out if that claim actually holds up. The short answer is that it does not, at least not in the way most people think.

The Question

After COVID, headlines were full of stories about tech salaries exploding and Silicon Valley workers getting rich while everyone else struggled with inflation. I wanted to look at the actual data and ask a simple question: did tech workers really come out ahead, and if so by how much?

Tools used: Python (Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn), FRED API, GitHub Codespaces

Data source: Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis (FRED) — sector level average hourly wages and CPI inflation data

The Data

I pulled 10 years of data from the Federal Reserve's free public API covering average hourly wages across five sectors and the Consumer Price Index for inflation. It is important to note that this data represents sector level monthly averages, not individual worker wages. That distinction matters and I will come back to it in the limitations section.

Here is exactly what each FRED series measures:

FRED Code What It Measures What It Includes
CPIAUCSL Consumer Price Index (Inflation) Weighted average price of a basket of goods and services for urban consumers including food, housing, transport, healthcare and clothing
CES5500000003 Tech and Information Wages Average hourly earnings for workers in the Information sector including software, telecommunications, data processing, publishing and broadcasting
CES6000000003 Professional Services Wages Average hourly earnings across Professional and Business Services including legal, accounting, management consulting, engineering, architecture, advertising and scientific research
CES6500000003 Healthcare Wages Average hourly earnings in Education and Health Services including hospitals, clinics, nursing homes, social assistance and educational institutions
CES4200000003 Retail Wages Average hourly earnings in Retail Trade including grocery stores, clothing retailers, electronics stores, car dealerships and general merchandise
CES0500000003 All Workers Average Average hourly earnings across all private sector workers in the US economy

What I Found

The data tells a more nuanced story than the popular narrative suggests.

Since September 2020, every sector lost purchasing power after inflation:

  • Inflation: +22.2%
  • Professional Services: +22.5% (barely broke even)
  • Healthcare: +21.9% (lost to inflation)
  • Tech and Information: +21.3% (also lost to inflation)
  • All Workers Average: +20.9% (lost to inflation)
  • Retail: +16.9% (hit the hardest)

Key finding: Tech workers lost 0.8% of real purchasing power since September 2020. Retail workers lost 5.2%. Everyone got poorer in real terms, just at different rates.

The most surprising chart was the pain period from 2021 to 2023. During the inflation peak, tech wage growth actually collapsed to around 2.5% while inflation was running at nearly 9%. Retail and healthcare workers had higher wage growth than tech workers during that specific window, largely because those workers could not be laid off remotely and had stronger bargaining power at the time.

The absolute dollar gap tells the inequality story more clearly:

  • Tech workers earn $46.47/hr on average
  • Professional Services: $43.31/hr
  • Healthcare: $35.01/hr
  • All Workers Average: $35.69/hr
  • Retail: $24.87/hr

The gap between tech and retail workers has grown to $21.60 per hour by 2024. Even when percentage growth rates look similar, a 1% loss on $46/hr hurts a lot less than a 5% loss on $25/hr. The inequality is not just about growth rates, it is baked into the absolute dollar difference.

A Note on the Popular Narrative

The headlines tend to focus on senior engineers at Google and Meta making $300K to $500K in total compensation. But those people are a tiny fraction of the tech workforce. The FRED data captures the average tech worker, the IT support person, the junior developer, the data analyst at a software company. Their story is much closer to everyone else's than the headlines suggest.

Limitations and Things to Consider

This data has real limitations that are worth understanding before drawing conclusions.

1. Sector level averages, not individual wages

The FRED data represents monthly averages across entire sectors. It does not show the distribution of wages within each sector. A sector average can look stable even when the gap between the highest and lowest earners within that sector is growing significantly.

2. The Professional Services result needs context

Professional Services barely beat inflation at +22.5% vs +22.2%. But this category includes lawyers, management consultants and engineers who are high billing rate professionals. Their wages likely pull the sector average up considerably. The experience of an average accountant or junior consultant within this category may look very different from the sector average suggests.

3. Overemployment is not accounted for

Post-COVID remote work enabled a phenomenon where some tech workers quietly hold two full time jobs simultaneously, sometimes called overemployment. If a portion of tech workers are earning two salaries, the FRED data would not capture that. The real individual income picture for some tech workers may be significantly higher than these sector averages show.

4. Total compensation vs hourly wages

This analysis looks at hourly wages only. Tech workers in particular often receive significant additional compensation through stock options, RSUs, and bonuses that are not captured in hourly wage data. For senior tech workers especially, total compensation can be multiples of base salary.

5. Geography matters

These are national averages. A tech worker in San Francisco or New York faces very different cost of living pressures than one in Edmonton or Austin. Geographic wage differences within sectors are significant and national averages smooth over that reality.
